ABOUT RESEARCH TEACHING SOCIAL IMPACT CONTACT

My research focuses on three core themes: regulation and governance of civil society organizations, inequality and elites in civil society and civil society development in hybrid regimes. One of my central questions of interest is how the state and capacity building organizations - through legal regulation, guidance and training - shape the internal governance of civil society organizations, affecting their leadership composition and representation. I am also interested in the reproduction of civil society elites and their contestation by grassroots and elite actors in long-lived democracies. Currently, I am developing a comparative research project on the democratic potential of civil society organizations in the Western Balkans.

The state largely defines the environments in which CSOs operate. This research theme focuses on the ways in which external pressures from the state affect different aspects of the governance of CSOs: internal democracy, professionalization, staff influence and board composition among others. The theme also encompasses studies in the different ways in which liberal democracies use legal regulation to constrain and enable CSO behaviour. Theoretically, it draws on debates in comparative politics, public policy, legal studies, and organisational sociology. Empirically, it focuses on long-lived democracies in Europe and diverse data including organisational case studies, interviews, archival research and organisational surveys.

Civil societies in Europe are undergoing increased hierarchization and resource stratification with some CSOs having disproportionate access to political and economic resources. Within this line of research, I have been studying mainly the composition (who are the civil society elites?), reproduction (how are they recruited, trained and legitimized?) and contestation of civil society elites (who and how is challenging their position of power?) in the UK, but also contributed to comparative studies in which UK is one of the cases. Theoretically, I have been drawing on debates in sociology of elites, social movement studies, management and leadership studies. Empirically, I have analysed prosopographic (biographical) data, interviews and documents.

Interest group systems in the Western Balkans have undergone process of maturation with the emergence of new interests and networks of CSOs. Under this theme I aim to assess the democratic potential of CSOs in the Western Balkans in light of three decades of civil society capacity development efforts by the EU and foreign donors. Simultaneously, I want to understand how illiberal and authoritarian tendencies typical for hybrid regimes affect the ability of CSOs to connect the citizens with the state. Theoretically, the study benefits from interest group research, Europeanization studies and literature on political regimes. Empirically, it draws on document analysis, case studies and organisational surveys.
